## Ideal Candidate Profile - Data Scientist - Midlands Partnership NHS Foundation Trust

Source role:
- `Data Scientist`
- `Midlands Partnership NHS Foundation Trust`
- <https://www.jobs.nhs.uk/candidate/jobadvert/C9301-26-0451?searchFormType=main&keyword=data&language=en>

### 1. What Kind of Candidate This Employer Is Actually Looking For

The ideal candidate for this role is not a narrow modelling specialist and not a dashboard-only analyst. The employer is looking for a production-minded healthcare data scientist who can operate across advanced analytics, data engineering, population health analytics, enterprise data quality, and governed AI delivery.

This person would likely present as someone who is comfortable moving between:
- predictive modelling and statistical analysis
- data extraction, transformation, and pipeline design
- NHS dataset integration and standards-aware analytics
- stakeholder-facing decision support
- data quality and governance
- technical leadership and mentoring

The employer is effectively looking for someone who can behave like a data scientist, an analytics engineer, a PHM analyst, and a responsible AI practitioner in one role, while still being able to explain outputs clearly to operational, clinical, and strategic audiences.

### 2. Core Candidate Shape

The matching candidate would likely describe themselves as someone who has built and deployed analytical solutions in complex healthcare or public-sector environments, not just experimented with models in isolation. Their story would show that they know how to work with messy, incomplete, multi-source operational and clinical data, and still produce outputs that are usable, governed, explainable, and decision-relevant.

They would likely have experience that sounds like this:
- building predictive, segmentation, forecasting, or risk models for operational or population-level decision-making
- integrating multiple health or service datasets into analytical pipelines
- improving data quality as part of analytical delivery rather than treating it as someone else's problem
- embedding code quality, documentation, version control, and reproducibility into analytical work
- turning model output into service-planning, risk, or intervention insight for non-technical stakeholders
- balancing innovation with governance, especially where AI or machine learning affects real services or service users

### 3. Requirement-to-Candidate Translation

#### A. If the employer wants advanced analytics and machine learning capability

The ideal candidate would be able to say that they have developed and deployed predictive and statistical models in real delivery settings, using tools such as `Python`, `R`, and `SQL`, and that those models were used for forecasting, segmentation, risk identification, or service improvement rather than remaining as exploratory prototypes.

The candidate's evidence would likely include:
- building predictive, descriptive, or prescriptive models
- selecting and evaluating appropriate modelling techniques
- working with feature engineering, model evaluation, and output interpretation
- explaining why particular modelling choices were made
- ensuring outputs were fair, reliable, and decision-appropriate

#### B. If the employer wants someone at the intersection of data science and data engineering

The ideal candidate would not speak only about analysis. They would also describe designing or maintaining analytical datasets, pipelines, transformations, and reproducible workflows. Their evidence would show they can work with engineers or directly perform some of the engineering needed to make analysis production-usable.

The candidate's evidence would likely include:
- extracting, cleaning, transforming, and linking large datasets from multiple systems
- building or maintaining pipelines using `SQL`, `Python`, `Azure Data Factory`, or `Databricks`
- documenting lineage, assumptions, and transformation logic
- using `Git`, notebooks, and platform tooling in a disciplined way
- collaborating closely with warehouse, engineering, or platform teams

#### C. If the employer wants Population Health Management capability

The ideal candidate would show that they understand population-level analysis, linked data, cohort identification, segmentation, and pathway or outcome analysis, especially in health or public-service contexts. They would sound like someone who can use data not only to describe activity, but to identify unmet need, prevention opportunities, and service-planning priorities.

The candidate's evidence would likely include:
- working with linked datasets across organisational or service boundaries
- identifying cohorts, risk groups, inequalities, or intervention opportunities
- supporting planning, early intervention, or pathway analysis with data
- aligning analytical work to external frameworks, standards, or system priorities

#### D. If the employer wants NHS data knowledge

The ideal candidate would likely have direct NHS or adjacent public-sector health analytics experience, or at minimum be able to demonstrate familiarity with NHS data structures, standards, and governance expectations. They would understand that in this environment, dataset meaning, definition quality, interoperability, and reporting consistency are not optional details.

The candidate's evidence would likely include:
- working with NHS datasets and flows
- using `NHS Data Dictionary` definitions or similar standards-led interpretation
- dealing with clinical, operational, and corporate data in one reporting or analytical environment
- understanding interoperability standards such as `FHIR`, `HL7`, or `OpenEHR`
- working within `GDPR`, NHS information governance, or equivalent controlled data environments

#### E. If the employer wants data quality ownership

The ideal candidate would present as someone who treats data quality as part of the analytical job. They would not frame poor data quality as a blocker only for others to solve. Instead, they would show that they can detect anomalies, reconcile inconsistencies, strengthen dataset fitness for use, and help embed validation and monitoring into analytical workflows.

The candidate's evidence would likely include:
- identifying anomalies and data gaps
- improving completeness, consistency, or accuracy in analytical datasets
- contributing to validation, reconciliation, or assurance processes
- working with platform and analytics teams to ensure trusted modelling inputs
- using analytical methods to support root-cause analysis of data quality issues

#### F. If the employer wants governed and explainable AI delivery

The ideal candidate would sound like someone who is enthusiastic about advanced analytics but not careless about risk. They would be able to describe how they worked within governance, ethics, explainability, and model-risk expectations, especially where outputs could affect operational or clinical decisions.

The candidate's evidence would likely include:
- understanding when AI or machine learning is appropriate and when it is not
- designing models and datasets that are fit for governed deployment
- documenting assumptions, limitations, and expected usage boundaries
- considering explainability, fairness, and operational risk in model design
- supporting or contributing to responsible AI assurance processes

#### G. If the employer wants dashboard integration and decision support

The ideal candidate would not stop at model building. They would also show that they can operationalise analytical outputs into reporting and decision-support surfaces such as `Power BI`, and that they know how to make outputs understandable to non-technical users.

The candidate's evidence would likely include:
- integrating model outputs into dashboards, scorecards, or reporting products
- presenting analysis through visualisations and narrative
- tailoring communication for clinicians, managers, executives, or cross-functional teams
- translating technical outputs into actions, priorities, or service recommendations

#### H. If the employer wants leadership and mentoring

The ideal candidate would present as someone who can lead technically even if they are not in a large formal management structure. They would likely have mentored analysts, guided technical quality, contributed to standards, or supported the growth of less experienced colleagues.

The candidate's evidence would likely include:
- mentoring analysts, developers, or junior technical staff
- reviewing work for quality and methodological soundness
- helping define good practice for analytical delivery
- contributing to team capability building and continuous improvement

### 4. The Candidate's Likely Strongest Claims

If this employer read an ideal candidate profile, the strongest claims would probably sound like this:

- I have delivered analytical models in environments where data quality, governance, and operational usefulness mattered as much as model performance.
- I can work across `Python`, `R`, `SQL`, cloud tooling, and reporting platforms to move from raw data to production-usable insight.
- I have worked with complex multi-source health or public-service data and can turn it into segmentation, forecasting, risk, or service-planning outputs.
- I understand that model delivery in healthcare requires explainability, ethics, documentation, and alignment to data standards.
- I can translate technical work into decisions for clinical, operational, and senior stakeholders.
- I can improve data quality and analytical reproducibility while building capability in the wider team.

### 5. The Employer's Implied Non-Negotiables

Even where not all of these are labelled as hard mandatory requirements in the pasted format, the role strongly implies that the ideal candidate must be credible in the following areas:

- strong hands-on analytical and statistical capability
- real comfort with `SQL` and at least one of `Python` or `R`
- ability to work with complex, messy, multi-source datasets
- confidence operating in governed environments
- ability to communicate to non-technical and senior audiences
- enough technical maturity to support production-ready analytics rather than one-off analysis
- enough domain maturity to operate responsibly in healthcare or public-service data settings

### 6. What Would Make a Candidate Look Especially Strong for This Role

The strongest-fit candidate would likely go beyond generic data science claims and show evidence of:
- NHS or health-system data experience
- PHM, health inequalities, or linked-data work
- cloud analytics exposure such as `Azure`, `Databricks`, `Fabric`, or `Azure ML`
- reproducible engineering practices such as version control and documented pipelines
- experience with interoperability, governance, and explainability
- stakeholder credibility with clinicians, operational leads, and senior management
- mentoring, guidance, or analytical leadership capability

### 7. Short Ideal-Candidate Portrait

The ideal candidate for this post is a healthcare-facing data scientist who can build models, engineer usable analytical datasets, improve data quality, and translate complex outputs into service action. They are technically credible, governance-aware, standards-conscious, and comfortable working across data science, analytics engineering, PHM, and stakeholder communication. They are not just being hired to analyse data. They are being hired to make advanced analytics operationally useful, trustworthy, and fit for real organisational decision-making.
