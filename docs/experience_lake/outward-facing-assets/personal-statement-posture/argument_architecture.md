# The structure I would actually recommend

For this kind of application question / supporting statement / cover-letter-equivalent, I would use a **5-part structure**.

Not because “templates are good,” but because each part answers a different hiring doubt.

## 1. Opening: Role-fit hook

**Purpose:** show immediately that you understand what this role really is and why you want it.

This paragraph answers:

* Why this post?
* Why this setting?
* Why you, for this kind of role?

It should do three things fast:

* name the role in its real shape
* show genuine motivation
* signal fit without dumping evidence yet

### What this paragraph should sound like

Not:

* “I am writing to apply…”
* “I meet the criteria…”
* “I am passionate about data…”

But:

* “This role particularly appeals to me because…”
* “What stands out to me about this post is…”
* “I am especially drawn to this opportunity because it sits at the intersection of…”

For Midlands, the opening should probably signal:

* advanced analytics
* governed data
* operational usefulness
* healthcare/public-service context

Because that is the real shape of the post. 

---

## 2. Core fit paragraph: strongest direct criteria

**Purpose:** establish that you are not speculative; you already match the technical core.

This paragraph answers:

* Can this person actually do the job?
* Are they worth shortlisting on technical grounds?

This is where your strongest direct-fit areas go:

* MSc / formal training
* predictive/statistical modelling
* SQL and Python
* reusable analytical datasets
* dashboards/tools
* data modelling / ML / cloud / APIs / Git
* explainability / governance
* non-technical communication. 

### Important rule

This paragraph should be **clustered**, not checklist-like.

So not:

> I meet the degree requirement. I meet the SQL requirement. I meet the Python requirement...

Instead:

> My recent work has given me strong experience across predictive and statistical modelling, reusable analytical datasets, SQL/Python-based analysis and automation, and stakeholder-facing reporting outputs...

That’s how multiple criteria get answered in one natural paragraph.

### Mini-structure inside the paragraph

Use this rhythm:
**capability cluster → brief evidence → role relevance**

Example shape:

* “My recent work has involved X, Y, and Z...”
* “This included...”
* “That aligns closely with your requirement for...”

That keeps it human.

---

## 3. Governed-setting paragraph: quality, explainability, and healthcare transfer

**Purpose:** show that you understand this is not just technical work; it is controlled, low-risk, accountable work.

This paragraph answers:

* Does this person understand the setting?
* Do they understand that healthcare analytics is governance-heavy?
* Are they thoughtful enough to be trusted?

This is where you speak to:

* data quality
* reconciliation / validation / assurance
* documentation
* explainability
* information governance / ethics
* decision-support, not just modelling
* healthcare/public-sector transfer

This paragraph is important because it is where you stop sounding like “another technical applicant” and start sounding like someone who understands the seriousness of the work.

### Critical nuance

This is also where your **adjacent fit** should be handled.

Not defensively.
Not apologetically.
But honestly.

Something like:

* while your recent work has not been in the NHS directly, it has been governance-heavy, multi-source, decision-critical, and documentation-driven
* that makes your transfer credible

That is much stronger than pretending NHS experience you do not have.

---

## 4. Distinctiveness paragraph: what sets you apart

**Purpose:** answer “why you rather than another technically plausible applicant?”

This paragraph answers:

* What is distinctive about this candidate?
* What do they offer beyond generic competence?
* Why should we remember them?

This is where your real differentiator goes.

And I do **not** think your differentiator should be:

> I built a fraud platform.

That is still too “thing-based.”

Your real differentiator is more like:

**You connect modelling, data quality, analytical preparation, explainability, and operational use as one delivery problem.**

That is much more role-relevant.

This paragraph can also briefly bring in:

* your evidence-first style
* your production-mindedness
* your low-risk posture
* your ability to make analysis usable, not just technically interesting

This is the paragraph where “what makes me different from my peers” belongs.

---

## 5. Close: credible, forward-looking close

**Purpose:** end with seriousness, not fluff.

This paragraph answers:

* Does this person genuinely want the post?
* Do they sound mature and appointable?
* Are they closing well?

The close should do three things:

* reaffirm fit
* reaffirm motivation
* show readiness to contribute and grow

Not:

> I look forward to hearing from you.

That is too empty on its own.

Better:

* “I would welcome the opportunity to bring this analytical and governance-focused approach into a healthcare setting...”
* “I believe I would bring strong direct capability in X and Y, together with a serious commitment to developing further in the areas specific to NHS practice...”

That close feels committed and honest.

# So what does the flow feel like?

Here is the overall emotional / argumentative movement:

### Paragraph 1

**I understand this role, and I genuinely want this type of post.**

### Paragraph 2

**I directly meet the technical core strongly.**

### Paragraph 3

**I understand the governed, healthcare-adjacent setting and am a credible transfer, not a random leap.**

### Paragraph 4

**Here is what is distinctive about how I work and why that matters to you.**

### Paragraph 5

**Appointing me would be a sensible, serious decision.**

That is the flow.

Not “intro-body-conclusion.”
A **selection argument** with momentum.

# How do you stop it sounding programmatic?

This is the art part you were talking about.

Three things stop it sounding mechanical:

## 1. Vary the function of paragraphs

Not every paragraph should “claim + evidence + relevance” in exactly the same rhythm.

For example:

* paragraph 1 is more interpretive and motivational
* paragraph 2 is more evidence-heavy
* paragraph 3 is more trust/governance-heavy
* paragraph 4 is more distinctive/personal
* paragraph 5 is more forward-looking

That variation gives it soul.

## 2. Use criteria clusters, not criteria lists

Never march through the person spec item by item unless the form explicitly forces that.

Instead, cluster them into natural themes:

* technical core
* governed delivery
* communication and decision support
* healthcare transfer
* strengths and distinctiveness

That makes it feel written by a person.

## 3. Let asymmetry show

Do not give equal space to:

* your MSc
* your lack of NHS experience
* your Five Guys mentoring
* your SQL/Python work

That would be absurd.

The statement should be **weighted**.
Your strongest fit gets the most space.
Your adjacent fit gets careful but shorter treatment.
Your weak areas get acknowledged briefly, not spotlighted.

That asymmetry makes it believable.

# So what is the practical “format” you can reuse?

This is the reusable pattern I’d recommend you carry forward across jobs:

## The reusable cover-letter / supporting-statement posture

### Opening

**What is this role really about, and why do I want this kind of work?**

### Core fit

**Which of the decisive criteria do I meet directly and strongly?**

### Trust / setting

**Do I understand the environment, and how do I show low risk where my fit is adjacent rather than literal?**

### Distinctiveness

**What is particular about how I work that makes me more valuable than a generic applicant?**

### Close

**Why is appointing me a sensible choice, and why am I serious about this post?**

That is the structure.

It is flexible enough for other roles, but sharp enough for this one.
