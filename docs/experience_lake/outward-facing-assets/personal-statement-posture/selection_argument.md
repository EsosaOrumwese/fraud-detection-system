Yes — this is the right place to slow down and get the **thinking posture** correct before writing anything.

I think the cleanest way to say it is this:

A resume says, **“this is the shape of my fit.”**
A supporting statement / application answer says, **“this is why appointing me is a sensible, low-risk decision for this specific role.”**

That is the argument.

Not:

* “here is everything I’ve built”
* “here is a longer version of my CV”
* “here is proof of every bullet”
* “here is a generic enthusiasm statement”

But:
**“Given what you need, given what I’ve actually done, and given where my fit is direct versus adjacent, here is why I am a strong, honest appointment.”**

For this Midlands post, that matters because the Trust is not hiring a narrow modeller. The role is explicitly a hybrid across advanced analytics, data engineering, enterprise data quality, governed AI, and stakeholder-facing decision support, with a person specification that mixes technical criteria, governance criteria, communication criteria, and values/behaviours.   

So the approach I would teach you is not a template. It is a **decision argument** built from a sequence of questions.

## 1. Start from the hidden hiring question

Before writing, ask:

**“Why does this employer need this person?”**

Not “what are the duties?”
Not “what keywords are in the ad?”
But: **what problem are they trying to solve by hiring?**

For Midlands, the answer is something like:

They need someone who can build and operationalise analytics responsibly in a healthcare setting — someone who can model, prepare and link data, improve data quality, explain outputs, support dashboards/reporting, and work in a governance-heavy environment without treating those as separate jobs.  

That is the **real call** underneath the criteria.

## 2. Turn the person specification into a fit map, not a checklist

Do **not** start writing from top to bottom of the person spec.

Instead, classify each criterion into one of three buckets:

**Green** = direct, strong fit
**Amber** = transferable / adjacent fit
**Red** = not claimable yet

For this role, your Greens are things like:

* MSc / formal training
* predictive and statistical modelling
* SQL and Python
* dashboards / reports / analytical tools
* data modelling, statistics, ML
* AWS SageMaker, APIs, Git
* data engineering / DevOps awareness
* explaining complex data to non-technical users
* information governance, ethics, AI explainability
* problem-solving, organisation, communication.  

Your Ambers are things like:

* continuous professional development
* NHS standards / governance awareness
* NHS data flows / definitions awareness
* multidisciplinary teamwork
* support for training / development
* cross-boundary collaboration. 

Your Reds are things like:

* direct NHS/public-sector health analytics experience
* population health analytics training
* Azure/Databricks certification
* formal analyst mentoring
* interoperability standards as applied experience
* publications / analytics networks. 

That fit map changes the writing completely, because now you know:

* what to **lead with**
* what to **frame carefully**
* what to **not pretend**

## 3. The statement is a “selection-risk memo”

This is the part I think matters most.

The hiring team is quietly asking:

* Can this person do the job?
* Do they understand the setting?
* Are they honest about their boundaries?
* Are they strong enough to justify an interview?
* Will they need too much interpretation or rescue from us?
* Do they actually care about this role, or are they mass-applying? 

So the statement should reduce **selection risk**.

That means every paragraph should lower one of their concerns.

For example:

**Concern 1:** Can he actually do the technical core?
Answer with your strongest direct-fit evidence.

**Concern 2:** Does he understand that this is governed healthcare analytics, not toy modelling?
Answer with your governance, documentation, data quality, explainability, and operational-use posture.

**Concern 3:** He has no NHS role history — is that a problem?
Answer honestly: direct NHS experience is adjacent rather than literal, but your recent work is already governance-heavy, standards-conscious, and decision-critical, so the transfer is real.

**Concern 4:** Why this role?
Answer with specific motivation toward healthcare/public-service impact and responsible analytics, not generic passion for data.

That is why I call it a **selection-risk memo** rather than a cover letter.

## 4. The writing should follow importance, not symmetry

You said something important: your fit is **asymmetrical**. Exactly.

That means the statement should not give equal weight to everything.

The mistake is to think:

* one sentence per criterion
* equal treatment for all criteria
* balanced coverage of everything

That creates dead, mechanical writing.

Instead, weight the answer like this:

### High-weight areas

These get the most space because they are both important to the role and strong in your evidence:

* predictive / statistical modelling
* SQL / Python
* reusable analytical datasets
* linked-data analysis
* data quality / reconciliation / assurance
* explainable / governed AI
* reporting / dashboard / decision support
* communication to non-technical stakeholders.      

### Medium-weight areas

These matter, but should be framed briefly and honestly:

* multidisciplinary collaboration
* CPD
* support for training / helping others
* NHS standards awareness
* public-service motivation. 

### Low-weight / boundary areas

These should be acknowledged only if useful, not over-explained:

* no direct NHS employment yet
* no PHM training
* no Azure/Databricks certification
* no formal analyst supervision
* no publications/networks. 

So the statement should be **deliberately uneven**.
That is a feature, not a flaw.

## 5. The exact questions you should ask while drafting

This is the part you said you wanted most.

When writing a supporting statement / cover letter / application answer, ask these in order:

### Question 1

**What is the employer really trying to buy with this hire?**

For Midlands: not “a person who knows Python.”
More like: “a production-minded data scientist who can build useful, governed, explainable analytics in a complex healthcare setting.”  

### Question 2

**Which 3–5 criteria are actually decisive?**

Not every criterion has equal weight.

Here, the decisive ones are probably:

* modelling
* SQL/Python
* data engineering / preparation
* data quality / governance
* explainability / communication
* dashboards / reporting. 

### Question 3

**Which parts of my evidence most clearly reduce doubt on those decisive criteria?**

This is where the slices come in.

Not to dump proof, but to choose your strongest trust-building evidence:

* predictive modelling slice
* linked population/pathway slice
* data-quality slice
* case-product slice
* governed AI slice
* dashboard slice.    

### Question 4

**Where is my fit adjacent rather than direct, and how do I frame that without weakening myself?**

This is critical.

Do not say:

* “I have NHS data experience” if you do not.
* “I know FHIR/HL7/OpenEHR” as applied experience if you do not.

Instead say:

* “My recent work has been in governance-heavy, decision-critical analytical delivery, which gives me a strong transferable base for regulated healthcare analytics.”
* “I am motivated to deepen my knowledge of NHS data standards and flows in practice.”

That is honest and low-risk.

### Question 5

**Why this role, specifically?**

Not “I’m passionate about data.”
Not “this aligns with my skills.”

Instead:

* why healthcare
* why governed analytics
* why this kind of post, where modelling and operational usefulness have to coexist. 

### Question 6

**What is distinctive about me in their language, not mine?**

Not “I built a fraud platform.”

Better:

* I connect modelling, data quality, reporting, and operational use
* I treat explainability and governance as part of delivery, not as afterthoughts
* I make analysis production-usable, not just technically interesting.   

### Question 7

**What would make me sound risky or inflated?**

Examples:

* pretending online reading equals applied NHS experience
* overclaiming deployment where your own evidence says “bounded” or “reviewable”
* turning the statement into a proof appendix
* claiming every desirable criterion. 

That question should actively shape what you remove.

## 6. When do claims come in?

Claims come in **after** you name the match problem.

Bad:

> I built X, Y, Z…

Better:

> This role requires someone who can combine modelling, reusable analytical datasets, data quality, and explainable decision support. My recent work aligns strongly with that because…

So the structure is:

1. name the criterion / role need
2. state your fit
3. give only enough evidence to make it believable
4. move on

That is the right balance between resume and proof doc.

## 7. How do you show honesty and low risk?

Not by sounding modest.
By sounding **precise**.

Low-risk candidates do three things:

* they **lead strongly** where the fit is direct
* they **frame adjacency cleanly** where the fit is not literal
* they **do not force false equivalence**

For example:

Strong:

> I have strong transferable experience in governance-heavy, multi-source analytical delivery and explainable decision-support outputs.

Risky:

> I have deep NHS data flow and standards knowledge.

The first sounds interview-safe.
The second sounds easy to challenge.

Low-risk writing is specific about:

* what you have done
* what you have not done
* why the transfer still makes sense

## 8. What keeps it from becoming a proof document?

This rule:

**A supporting statement explains fit. It does not attempt to prove every claim exhaustively.**

So each paragraph should stop once the hiring team can think:
**“Yes, that sounds credible.”**

You do **not** need:

* every metric
* every slice detail
* every tool
* every nuance of the platform

That all lives backstage.

The statement only needs enough evidence to support selection.

## 9. What keeps it from sounding AI-generated or soulless?

Three things:

### First: motive must be real

You need one or two sentences that are genuinely about why **this kind of work** matters to you.

Not sentimental fluff — just real direction.

For example:

* you want your analytical work to matter in environments where governance, usefulness, and impact are inseparable
* you are specifically drawn to healthcare/public-service settings because the consequences of data quality and interpretability are real

### Second: asymmetry must be visible

A real human answer does not treat every criterion equally.
It clearly leans into strengths and handles gaps briefly.

### Third: boundary language must be human

Phrases like:

* “while my recent work has not been within the NHS…”
* “I would bring a strong transferable base…”
* “I am motivated to deepen that knowledge in practice…”

That is what makes it sound honest rather than mass-produced.

## 10. So what is the actual approach?

If I had to compress all of this into one sentence:

**The supporting statement should be a criterion-led, trust-building selection argument that shows why you are a strong and honest fit for this specific post, using your strongest evidence where the fit is direct and clean transfer language where the fit is adjacent.**

That is the posture.

Not template-first.
Not proof-first.
Not ego-first.

**Selection-first.**

And if I had to reduce it even further, the internal question you should ask every time is:

**“Why is appointing me to this role a sensible decision, given what they need and what I have actually done?”**

That is the real writing question.
