## 1. Requirement Being Targeted

The requirement being targeted here is the Money and Pensions Service `Data Insight Analyst` expectation around:
- identifying emerging risks through investigation
- performing root-cause analysis on customer, quality, or service data
- highlighting trends or improvement opportunities before they become larger problems
- helping teams interpret what the signals mean in practice

This maps directly to the candidate-profile requirement that the analyst should be able to sound like someone who:
- does not stop at reporting status
- can investigate why something is happening
- can identify early warning indicators rather than only late-stage symptoms
- can turn that explanation into more informed intervention logic

This note exists because that requirement is easy to flatten into the vague statement:
`I identify risks and explain what they mean`

That is not the real burden.

The employer is asking for something sharper:
- one early warning signal
- one investigated likely cause chain
- one bounded root-cause or driver reading
- one practical implication for intervention or improvement

## 2. The Analogue On The Current Platform

The current platform does not contain a literal Money and Pensions Service `CX&Q` risk register, live service-investigation unit, or full causal-analysis environment, so the slice has to use an honest analogue.

The closest truthful analogue is:
- one mixed-source reporting lane already established in Money and Pensions Service `3.B`
- one governed output surface already established in Money and Pensions Service `3.A`
- one structured-plus-narrative evidence pack already established in Money and Pensions Service `3.D`
- one root-cause investigation posture already proven methodologically in HUC `03`

So for this slice:
- `emerging risk` maps to `one concentrated shared-focus signal within the governed Money and Pensions Service lane`
- `root-cause analysis` maps to `one bounded investigation of likely drivers across the already-controlled evidence surfaces`
- `what the signals mean in practice` maps to `one intervention-relevant note that stays proportional to the evidence and avoids fake certainty`

In practice, that means the default posture should be:
- `likely-driver` first
- `root-cause-style` second
- singular `root cause` only if the evidence behaves with the same clarity as the HUC discrepancy case

The claim must therefore remain:
- risk-and-investigation shaped
- likely-driver shaped
- platform-truth bounded
- careful not to imply a full causal model, predictive risk engine, or formal operational investigation authority where this slice only proves one bounded risk-and-root-cause pack

- early warning

## 7. Execution Posture

Execution should stay:
- risk-first
- investigation-first
- likely-driver-first
- bounded-intervention-first

Execution should not drift into:
- generic “risks and opportunities” wording with no investigation substance
- fake certainty about causality
- a predictive-risk or modelling claim
- broad service-improvement ownership

The first pass should:
- start from the existing shared focus signal in the governed Money and Pensions Service lane
- test whether one defensible early warning signal can be stated cleanly
- test whether one likely cause chain can be traced from the existing evidence surfaces
- only then write the intervention implication in practical language

That execution order matters because:
- the risk signal must exist before any cause language is written
- the likely driver chain must be defensible before any improvement implication is stated
- readability should only be tightened after the logic and limits are fixed

If the cause story remains plural or conditional after investigation, keep it that way.
This slice does not need a singular deterministic cause to be valid.

### 9.2 Lens 03 - Protect the investigation from weak evidence

Execution should:

3. narrow or stop if the evidence would force speculative causality

### 9.3 Lens 05 - Build the likely-driver and intervention reading

Execution should:
1. test one bounded likely cause chain
2. state what is most likely happening, not what is absolutely proven

Execution should:
1. make the risk and likely-driver wording readable
2. state what the signals mean in practice
3. avoid overclaiming certainty, ownership, or organisational authority
