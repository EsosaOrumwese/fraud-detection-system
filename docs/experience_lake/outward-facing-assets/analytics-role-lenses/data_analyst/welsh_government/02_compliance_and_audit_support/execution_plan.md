## 8. Traceability Strategy

The scrutiny story has to stay honest.

The first-pass traceability strategy should answer:
- what exactly is authoritative?
- how does the released view connect back to the governing rule?
- how do the release checks protect that authority?
- why does the slice stop at audit-support rather than stronger compliance ownership?

Allowed posture:
- one bounded audit-readiness surface
- one explicit rule-authority or traceability surface
- one defensibility note
- one repeatable review-check pack

Disallowed posture:
- claiming statutory authority
- asserting full compliance-function ownership
- importing generic compliance language as fake proof

Decision rule:
- every retained element must earn its place in either:
  - audit-readiness
  - traceability
  - defensibility
  - recurring review support
- if an element does not support one of those purposes, drop it

## 9. Defensibility Strategy

This is what makes the slice useful rather than merely administrative.

- why can a reviewer trust this released view?
- what controls make the output review-ready?
- what should a manager or reviewer now be able to rely on more safely?

- use the traceability surface to make that use clearer or safer
- keep the consequence proportional to the evidence

Disallowed posture:

- claiming wider statutory authority
- claiming organisation-wide audit consequences not evidenced by the pack
